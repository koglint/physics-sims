Formula Derivation

Ideal Banked Curve Speed

Back to simulation
Step 1

Resolve the normal force into components on the banked road.

Split FN into horizontal and vertical components that match the axes used in the road-view diagram.

This is the key starting idea because the car is moving in a horizontal circle, not diagonally through the road. Once the force is broken into components, each part can be matched to a specific type of motion.

Step 2

FNy = m g

The vertical component of the normal force balances the car’s weight, so there is no vertical acceleration.

The car does not rise off the road or sink into it, so the total vertical force must be zero. That means the upward vertical part of the normal force must exactly equal the downward gravitational force.

Step 3

FNx = m v2 / r

The horizontal component of the normal force points toward the centre of the turn and provides the centripetal force.

Circular motion always requires a net inward force, even when speed is constant. Here, that inward force comes from the horizontal part of the normal reaction from the road.

Step 4

FNx = FN sin θ and FNy = FN cos θ

Using the force triangle, FNx is opposite θ and FNy is adjacent, so the components can be written with sine and cosine.

This step converts the force diagram into trig relationships. Writing the components in terms of FN and θ lets us connect the geometry of the banked road to the force equations from the previous steps.

Step 5

(FNx / FNy) = (FN sin θ / FN cos θ) = (m v2 / r) / (m g)

Divide the horizontal equation by the vertical equation so the component ratio matches a trig ratio directly.

Using equation 1, FN cos θ = mg, and equation 2, FN sin θ = mv2/r, we take equation 2 divided by equation 1. That gives (FN sin θ) / (FN cos θ) = (mv2/r) / (mg).

This is a smart algebra step because it removes quantities that would otherwise be awkward to solve for separately. By dividing one whole equation by the other, the unknown normal force appears in both top and bottom and is set up to cancel.

Step 6

tan θ = v2 / (r g)

FN cancels from the left side and m cancels from the right side, leaving a tangent relationship.

Because sin θ / cos θ = tan θ, the trig side simplifies neatly as well. This is the point where the bank angle is linked directly to the speed needed for the turn.

Step 7

v2 = r g tan θ

Rearrange the equation by multiplying both sides by rg so that v2 is isolated.

This step is just solving the equation for the quantity we care about. Since we want a formula for speed, we first isolate v2 before taking the square root in the final step.

Step 8

v = √(r g tan θ)

Taking the square root gives the final expression for the ideal speed on a frictionless banked curve.

This result shows that the ideal speed depends on the curve radius, gravity, and the bank angle. In this model, friction is not needed because the tilt of the road alone provides the correct centripetal force.
